Why this math is different

A salaried job hides its true costs. Your employer pays half your payroll taxes, your health insurance, your laptop, your software, and — crucially — they pay you for the hours you spend in meetings, on email, and learning new skills.

As a freelancer, every one of those costs lands on your invoice or it lands on you. Most independent workers can genuinely bill only 50–60% of a working week once admin, proposals, and marketing are subtracted. That's why this calculator divides your revenue target by billable hours, not working hours — and why the result is usually 1.5–2× what your salaried hourly rate used to be.

Use the number on the receipt as your floor, not your price. Quote fixed project fees to clients, estimate hours privately, and never let the effective rate dip below your floor.

Frequently asked questions

How do I calculate my freelance hourly rate?

Add your target annual income, yearly business expenses, and a tax reserve, then divide by your true billable hours — the hours you can actually invoice after admin, marketing, and time off. This calculator does exactly that, plus a safety margin for slow months.

Why is my rate so much higher than my old salary suggested?

You now pay both halves of payroll tax, your own insurance and equipment, and you absorb every unbillable hour. A rule of thumb: a freelance rate needs to be roughly 1.5–2× your old salaried hourly equivalent just to break even.

Should I charge hourly or per project?

Keep your hourly rate as a private floor and quote fixed project prices. Estimate the hours, multiply by your floor rate, and add 15–25% for revisions and scope creep. Clients prefer fixed prices, and you capture the upside of working efficiently.

What counts as a billable hour?

Only time you can put on an invoice: client work, client meetings, deliverable revisions. Proposals, bookkeeping, marketing, learning, and email are real work — but nobody pays for them directly, which is precisely why your billable hours must.

Is my data stored anywhere?

No. Everything runs in your browser. Nothing you type leaves your device, and there's no account, cookie wall, or email gate.
